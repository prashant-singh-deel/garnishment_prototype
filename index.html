1. Problem statement

A Deel client has a US-based independent contractor workforce where some contractors have outstanding financial obligations — including court-ordered child support, IRS tax levies, and commercial debt assignments under UCC § 9-406. The client needs to ensure these obligations are honored as part of the contractor payment cycle.

Deel currently has no product capability to support this. The sales team has committed to delivering this as part of the client deal. Without it, Deel cannot retain this client and will have no scalable answer for future clients who face the same requirement.

Important distinction: The sample document provided by the client is a UCC § 9-406 commercial receivables assignment — not a court-issued wage garnishment. These are different legal instruments with different obligations, verification paths, and product requirements. This brief covers both, clearly separated.

Deel's legal position

Deel acts as a payment agent — the client funds Deel, and Deel disburses to the IC. For government-issued orders (IWOs, IRS levies), Deel may already be a legally obligated "income withholder" as the entity that disburses funds. For UCC § 9-406 notices, the obligation sits primarily with the client (the account debtor), and Deel executes on the client's instruction.

Deel is not building a payroll garnishment product. It's building a payment instruction execution layer that processes legally required or client-directed payment assignments from the IC's own Deel account.

Misclassification risk

Processing payment assignments from the IC's own Deel balance — rather than withholding at the point of disbursement — keeps Deel's role clearly as a payment account rather than an employer withholding wages. Key signals to avoid: withholding from IC payments before disbursement, CCPA disposable income calculations, using "wage garnishment" in IC-facing copy, receiving directives directly from assignees, and doing new hire reporting on the client's behalf.

About the sample document (UCC § 9-406)

The document provided is a commercial receivables assignment from East Shore Equities SMB, LLC (a Merchant Cash Advance company) directed at LaserShip, Inc. D/B/A OnTrac Final Mile. The IC — Jenniffer Planet LLC — voluntarily assigned her future receivables as collateral for a cash advance. East Shore filed a UCC-1 Financing Statement with the Ohio Secretary of State on February 2, 2026 (FS# OH00296077906). Total assigned amount: $19,447.38.

3. Proposed solution

Build a Payment Assignment Directives module within the existing IC and CoR payment infrastructure.

Core mechanic: Deel disburses the full invoice amount to the IC's Deel account as normal, ring-fences the portion subject to the directive, then fires an automatic outgoing payment to the designated authority or assignee. The IC receives their remaining balance. The payment originates from the IC's own Deel account — not as a deduction by Deel or the client.

4. Product flow

IC raises invoice Unchanged
IC raises invoice to client as normal. No directive logic at this stage.
2
Client approves invoice Modified
Client approves as normal. If an active directive exists for this IC, an informational banner is shown: "This contractor has an active payment directive. $X of this payment will be automatically paid to [authority] after disbursement." No action required from client at this step.
3
Client pays Deel Unchanged
Client pays Deel the full invoice amount as normal. No change to payment method, amount, or timing.
4
Client submits payment directive New
Client uploads the directive document (IWO, IRS levy notice, or UCC § 9-406 notice) in the new Payment Directives section. Client selects the affected contractor, enters directive details (assignee name, total amount, payment method preference), and submits for review. No payment is redirected at this point.
5
Deel verifies the directive New
Deel ops reviews within 3 business days. For IWOs and tax levies: verifies issuing authority and form validity. For UCC § 9-406 notices: confirms UCC-1 filing in public state records, document consistency, and assignee legitimacy. Assignee onboarded as verified payee (KYB + sanctions). Invalid directives rejected; payments continue to IC normally.
6
IC acknowledges the directive New
IC receives in-platform notification and email in plain language. 5 business days to respond. Accept → directive activates next cycle. Dispute → paused, Deel steps back entirely, payments continue normally. No response after 5 days → activates by default (legal to confirm).
7
Deel credits IC's Deel account — full invoice amount Unchanged
Deel disburses the full approved invoice amount to the IC's Deel account as normal. This step does not change.
8
Ring-fence activates New
Immediately upon credit, the directive amount is ring-fenced — not available for withdrawal. IC's balance shows: Total balance, Reserved (payment directive), and Available to withdraw separately. Ring-fence cannot be released until the automatic payment settles.
9
Automatic payment fires to authority / assignee New
Ring-fenced amount remitted to verified payee (SDU, IRS EFTPS, assignee bank). Success: ring-fence released, both parties receive Payment Disbursement Advice. Failure: ring-fence maintained, 3 retries over 5 business days, ops escalation if all fail.
10
Directive termination New
Auto-terminates on: (a) balance satisfied, (b) written release from assignee, (c) IC dispute upheld, or (d) contract ends — Deel sends formal closure notice to assignee, remaining balance is between IC and assignee.

5. Key design decisions

Decision 1 — Do not show directive amounts on the IC's invoice

The IC's invoice states what the client owes for services rendered. Showing a directive as a line item makes it look like a payslip with a deduction — a misclassification signal. Directive amounts appear only in the Payment Disbursement Advice, separate from the invoice.

Decision 2 — Use "payment assignment directive," not "garnishment"

"Wage garnishment" is an employment law term. All product UI, notifications, and documentation should use payment assignment directive or income withholding order (for IWOs specifically).

Decision 3 — Deel acts only on client instruction, never unilaterally

Assignees cannot submit directives directly to Deel. The notice must come through the client. If an assignee contacts Deel directly claiming authority under a Power of Attorney, Deel refers them to the client.

Decision 4 — Directives don't apply to in-flight invoices until IC acknowledges

Invoices already approved at submission time are not subject to the directive until IC acknowledgment is complete. Invoices credited before the directive was received are not subject to it at all.

Decision 5 — No CCPA disposable income calculations for ICs

CCPA exemption floors are an employee concept. For ICs, Deel follows the order as written. For UCC § 9-406, the full payment is typically redirected until balance is satisfied.

Decision 6 — V1 scope is fixed contracts only for ICs

Fixed contracts map cleanly to IWO periods and assignment amounts. Pay-as-you-go and milestone contracts require service-period attribution logic not currently in Deel's infrastructure. These are Phase 2.

9. Scope

In scope — v1

  • Income Withholding Orders (IWOs)
  • IRS and state tax levies
  • UCC § 9-406 commercial assignments
  • US only
  • IC fixed contracts only
  • CoR — all directive types

Out of scope — v1

  • Consumer debt garnishments for ICs
  • PAYG and milestone contract types
  • Non-US directives
  • Automated multi-directive priority logic
  • Direct assignee submission
  • Cross-contract balance sweeping

10. Phasing

Phase 1
CoR — fastest to market
Deel is already employer of record. Full directive support — IWOs, IRS levies, consumer debt. Satisfies the immediate client deal if contractors are on CoR.
Phase 2
IC — fixed contracts
IWOs, IRS levies, UCC § 9-406. Fixed contracts only. Ring-fence mechanic, automatic wallet payment. Legal review across CA, TX, NY, FL before launch.
Phase 3
Expand IC scope
PAYG and milestone contracts. Automated multi-directive priority logic. Consumer debt for CO and VA.

11. Open questions

  1. Is Deel the "account debtor" under UCC § 9-406, or is the client? This determines whether Deel has a direct legal obligation or is executing as a service on the client's behalf.
  2. What is the correct default when the IC does not respond to the acknowledgment notification within 5 business days?
  3. For IRS levies with no partial release, what is Deel's obligation if the levy would result in 100% redirection? Does Deel need to notify the IC before executing?
  4. For the initial client deal — are contractors on standard IC contracts or CoR contracts?
  5. What is Deel's liability exposure for IWOs and IRS levies that may have been served on Deel before this product was built?
  6. Does Deel's current wallet infrastructure support atomic ring-fencing at the point of credit, or does this require new wallet architecture?

12. What this product does not do

  • Deel does not adjudicate disputes between ICs and assignees or authorities
  • Deel does not verify the underlying debt — only that the directive document is valid and the filing exists
  • Deel does not accept directives directly from assignees — all instructions come through the client
  • Deel does not apply CCPA disposable income calculations to ICs
  • Deel does not do new hire reporting on behalf of clients as part of this feature
  • Deel does not enforce MCA default remedies (e.g., Power of Attorney provisions) on behalf of assignees
  • Deel does not market this capability as "payroll garnishment" in any IC-facing context
